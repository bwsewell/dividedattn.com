We just changed the clocks again.  I lost an hour of sleep, my kid's schedule is wrecked, and the microwave has been wrong for three days because I keep forgetting to fix it.  This happens twice a year.  Every year.  And for what?

The United States needs to pick a time and stick with it.  Permanent daylight saving time is the answer.  The data overwhelmingly supports it.  The public overwhelmingly wants it.  The Senate unanimously passed it.  And yet here we are, still doing this idiotic dance with our clocks every March and November.

I'm going to lay out the case.  With numbers.

## People Literally Die

This isn't hyperbole.  The Monday after we "spring forward," heart attacks spike **24%** according to a [University of Michigan study](https://newsroom.heart.org/news/heres-your-wake-up-call-daylight-saving-time-may-impact-your-heart-health).  The American Heart Association has documented an uptick in strokes for two days after the transition.  A [Stanford study published in PNAS in 2025](https://med.stanford.edu/news/all-news/2025/09/daylight-saving-time.html) estimated that eliminating clock changes could prevent **220,000 strokes** and **1.7 million cases of obesity** under permanent DST.

Then there are the car crashes.  A [University of Colorado Boulder study](https://www.colorado.edu/today/2020/01/30/spring-forward-daylight-saving-time-brings-surge-deadly-crashes) analyzed over 700,000 fatal crashes from 1996 to 2017 and found a **6% spike** in the week after the spring change.  Over 22 years, that adds up to **627 people dead** from crashes directly attributable to the spring time shift.

Six hundred and twenty-seven people.  Because we moved the clocks.

## It's Expensive Too

[Chmura Economics](https://www.chmura.com/blog/cost-of-dst) estimated the total economic cost of the biannual time change at **$672 million annually** — heart attacks, strokes, workplace injuries, and traffic accidents combined.  Lost productivity estimates range from **$430 million to $1.7 billion per year** depending on methodology, including the time spent resetting clocks, updating systems, and dealing with the population-wide jet lag.

We are paying hundreds of millions of dollars a year to make ourselves sick, tired, and less productive.

## More Daylight = Less Crime

This one surprised me.  A study by Jennifer Doleac (University of Virginia) and Nicholas Sanders (Cornell), published in the *Review of Economics and Statistics*, found that robbery rates dropped **51%** during the sunset hour after the spring shift to DST.  Full-day robbery rates fell **7%**.  [Reported murder dropped 48% and rape 56%](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/fighting-crime-with-daylight-saving-time/) during the sunset hour.

Criminals don't like witnesses, and witnesses are more likely to be around when it's light out.  The study estimated that the 2007 three-week DST extension alone saved **$246 million** in social costs from avoided robberies.

More evening daylight makes people safer.  This isn't a theory.  It's measured.

## The Energy Argument is Dead

The original justification for DST was energy savings.  It doesn't hold up anymore.  When Indiana adopted statewide DST in 2006, researchers found residential electricity consumption actually **increased by about 1%**, costing households [$9 million per year](https://resources.environment.yale.edu/kotchen/pubs/revDSTpaper.pdf).  Reduced lighting demand was offset by more air conditioning and heating.

In modern, air-conditioned America, the energy savings from DST are negligible to nonexistent.  That argument is over.

## Your Mental Health Suffers

A [Danish study of 185,419 hospital contacts](https://www.psychiatrictimes.com/view/daylight-savings-time-frequently-asked-questions-and-answers-psychiatrists) found an **11% increase in depressive episodes** after the fall clock change, taking about 10 weeks to dissipate.  Research using U.S. mortality data found the suicide rate rises **6.25%** after the spring transition.

Meanwhile, more evening daylight means more time for outdoor recreation, exercise, and social activity after work — all protective factors against depression.  People don't go for walks in the dark.

## Almost Nobody Wants to Keep Switching

The polling on this is as close to unanimous as American public opinion gets:

- **75%** of Americans support ending the biannual clock change ([Stetson University, 2025](https://www2.stetson.edu/today/2025/03/majority-of-americans-prefer-year-round-daylight-saving-time-stetson-survey-reveals/))
- Only **12%** of U.S. adults actively support the current system ([AP-NORC](https://www.usnews.com/news/health-news/articles/2025-10-30/heres-how-americans-feel-about-changing-the-clocks-according-to-a-new-ap-norc-poll))
- Among those who want a permanent time, more prefer DST over standard time ([YouGov](https://yougov.com/en-us/articles/54237-the-times-they-are-a-changin-but-most-americans-would-rather-they-not))

Twelve percent.  We are doing this for twelve percent of the country.

## We Almost Fixed This

In March 2022, the Senate passed the [Sunshine Protection Act](https://www.congress.gov/bill/117th-congress/senate-bill/623/cosponsors) **by unanimous consent**.  Bipartisan.  Marco Rubio and Ed Markey.  18 cosponsors across both parties.  The bill would've made DST permanent nationwide.

Then it went to the House and died.  Not because anyone voted against it, but because the House couldn't decide whether permanent DST or permanent standard time was better.  They asked the Department of Transportation for an impact report and then just... stopped.

The bill has been reintroduced in [2023](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/senate-bill/29) and [2025](https://www.congress.gov/bill/119th-congress/house-bill/139).  Still waiting.

**19 states** have already passed legislation to adopt permanent DST.  They're all sitting there, ready to go, waiting for Congress to authorize it.  Arizona and Hawaii already don't observe DST (they use permanent standard time, which is allowed under current federal law).  The rest of us need an act of Congress.

## "But What About Dark Mornings?"

This is the strongest counterargument, so let me address it directly.  Under permanent DST, some northern cities would see sunrise as late as 9:00-9:30 AM in December.  Critics worry about kids walking to school in the dark.

Fair concern.  But kids in many northern cities already commute in darkness during standard time.  School start times can be adjusted — the American Academy of Pediatrics already recommends later start times for adolescents anyway.  And the net safety math favors evening light: 627 deaths from spring-forward crashes and dramatic crime reduction in evening hours outweigh the morning darkness risk, especially with modern LED streetlighting and crosswalk infrastructure.

The U.S. tried permanent DST briefly in 1974 and repealed it after complaints about dark mornings.  But that was during an energy crisis, with minimal public education, no LED streetlights, and the internet didn't exist to coordinate schedule changes.  It's not 1974 anymore.

## "But Russia Tried It and Gave Up"

Russia adopted permanent DST in 2011 and switched to permanent standard time in 2014.  This gets cited a lot.  But Russia spans **11 time zones** and extends to extreme northern latitudes where winter sunrise can be 10-11 AM.  The continental U.S. sits between 25 and 49 degrees latitude with far less extreme seasonal variation.

Turkey adopted permanent DST in 2016 and has kept it.  [Most of the world doesn't observe DST at all](https://www.pewresearch.org/short-reads/2023/10/26/most-countries-dont-observe-daylight-saving-time/).  The EU tried to abolish it after **84% of 4.6 million respondents** voted to end it, but implementation has stalled in bureaucracy.  Sound familiar?

## Just Do It

This shouldn't be hard.  The health data is clear.  The economic data is clear.  The crime data is clear.  The public opinion is clear.  The Senate already voted unanimously.  Nineteen states are sitting there with legislation ready to activate.

We change our clocks twice a year because of a law written in 1966.  It made questionable sense then.  It makes zero sense now.  Pick daylight saving time.  Make it permanent.  Stop killing people over an hour.

Congress managed to agree on this once.  They need to finish the job.
